Understanding Your Electricity Bill

Before you can calculate solar savings, you need to understand the two key components of your current electricity costs. Your bill is made up of a daily supply charge (typically 80 cents to $1.20 per day regardless of how much you use) and a usage charge (the rate per kilowatt-hour for the electricity you consume). Solar does not reduce your supply charge — it reduces your usage charge by replacing grid electricity with self-generated power.

Most households in {{SUBURB}} are on either a flat-rate tariff (typically 28–38 cents per kWh) or a time-of-use tariff (with peak rates of 40–55 cents in the evening, and off-peak rates around 15–18 cents overnight). The higher your usage tariff, the more valuable each kilowatt-hour of solar you consume directly. If you are on time-of-use pricing with high evening peak rates, self-consumption during the day is particularly valuable.

Typical Savings for a {{SUBURB}} Home

A 6.6 kW solar system installed in {{SUBURB}} will generate approximately 9,500–10,500 kWh of electricity per year depending on roof orientation and panel quality. A typical four-person household uses 18–22 kWh per day, or roughly 6,500–8,000 kWh per year.

The critical variable is self-consumption — how much of the solar you generate is actually used inside your home rather than exported to the grid at the much lower feed-in tariff rate. For a household that is home during the day or runs appliances on timers, a self-consumption rate of 35–45% is realistic without a battery. Here is what that looks like in dollar terms at a grid tariff of 32 cents per kWh and a feed-in tariff of 7 cents per kWh:

  • Annual solar generation: 10,000 kWh
  • Self-consumed (40%): 4,000 kWh × $0.32 = $1,280 saved on avoided grid purchases
  • Exported (60%): 6,000 kWh × $0.07 = $420 earned as feed-in credits
  • Total annual benefit: approximately $1,700

Households with pool pumps, air conditioning, or electric vehicle charging that can be shifted to daylight hours can comfortably exceed $2,000 per year in savings with the same system.

The Self-Consumption Equation

Self-consumption is the single biggest lever you have to improve your solar return, and it costs nothing to optimise. Every extra kilowatt-hour you consume directly from your solar — rather than exporting it for 7 cents and then buying it back for 32 cents — is worth an extra 25 cents in your pocket.

Practical steps to maximise self-consumption in a {{SUBURB}} home:

  • Run the dishwasher, washing machine, and clothes dryer between 10am and 3pm on sunny days using delay-start timers.
  • Set your pool pump to run during solar peak hours rather than overnight.
  • Pre-cool or pre-heat your home using solar power in the afternoon before the evening peak.
  • Charge an electric vehicle at home during the middle of the day.
  • Switch to a solar diverter for your hot water system — excess solar that would otherwise be exported heats your water tank instead.

Adding a Battery — Does It Double Savings?

A home battery captures the solar you would otherwise export during the day and makes it available in the evening, lifting your self-consumption rate from 35–45% to approximately 60–70%. In dollar terms for our example household above, that shift produces an additional $400–$600 per year in savings compared to solar alone.

A quality 10 kWh home battery adds approximately $8,000–$10,000 to the installed cost. At $500 per year in additional savings, the battery's own payback period is around 16–20 years on financial grounds alone. This is why we are honest with {{SUBURB}} customers: if your primary goal is maximising financial return, optimise your self-consumption habits first. If you want energy independence, protection from blackouts, or you have a time-of-use tariff with very high peak rates, a battery has a stronger case.
