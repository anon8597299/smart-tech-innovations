Start with Diversification

Diversification is the foundational principle of sound investing: spread your capital across different asset classes, geographies, and sectors so that a downturn in one area does not devastate your overall portfolio. In practice, a diversified portfolio might include:

  • Australian shares (exposure to domestic growth and franked dividends)
  • International shares (broader market access, including US technology and global leaders)
  • Fixed income or bonds (lower volatility, income, and a buffer in market downturns)
  • Property (directly or via listed real estate investment trusts)
  • Cash or cash equivalents (liquidity and stability)

The right mix depends on your investment timeframe and risk tolerance. A 35-year-old with a 25-year horizon can typically afford far more growth exposure than someone who retires in five years.

Growth vs Income Assets

A useful mental framework is to distinguish between growth assets — those that increase in value over time, such as shares and property — and income assets — those that generate regular cash flow, such as bonds, dividend shares, and term deposits.

In the accumulation phase (building wealth before retirement), most investors benefit from a growth-heavy allocation, reinvesting income to accelerate compounding. As you approach retirement, shifting toward income-generating assets provides more predictable cash flow to fund living expenses. Getting this balance right for your specific stage of life is something a financial adviser can help model in detail.

Managed Funds vs ETFs

Two of the most accessible investment vehicles for individual investors are managed funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs), and they are often compared directly.

Managed funds are actively managed by professional fund managers who select securities with the goal of outperforming a benchmark index. They typically carry higher fees (management expense ratios of 0.5%–1.5% or more) and require a minimum investment. Some active managers deliver consistent outperformance over time, but many do not — and higher fees compound against you just as returns do.

ETFs are passively managed funds that track an index (such as the ASX 200 or the S&P 500) and trade on the stock exchange like ordinary shares. Their primary advantages are low fees (often 0.03%–0.20% per year), broad diversification, transparency, and liquidity. For many investors, a core portfolio of low-cost, broad-market ETFs is a highly efficient foundation — with active funds or direct shares added selectively based on specific objectives.

Direct Property Considerations

Direct property investment is a common choice among Australian professionals and can form part of a sound long-term strategy. However, it requires careful consideration of several factors:

  • Concentration risk: A single investment property represents a large, undiversified exposure to one asset in one location.
  • Leverage: Using borrowed funds magnifies both gains and losses and introduces interest rate risk.
  • Illiquidity: Property cannot be partially sold — if you need cash, your options are limited.
  • Ongoing costs: Rates, maintenance, property management fees, insurance, and vacancy periods all erode net returns.

For some investors, residential or commercial property complements a share portfolio well. For others, the capital locked in a single property would be more efficiently deployed inside super or across a diversified portfolio. The right answer depends on your full financial picture.

Dollar Cost Averaging

Dollar cost averaging (DCA) involves investing a fixed amount at regular intervals — say, $1,000 every month — regardless of market conditions. When prices are high, your fixed amount buys fewer units. When prices fall, it buys more. Over time, this averages out your entry price and removes the temptation to time the market.

DCA is particularly well-suited to busy professionals who have regular income but limited time to monitor markets. Setting up an automatic investment schedule into a diversified ETF portfolio is one of the most practical and psychologically sustainable investment habits available.

Tax-Efficient Investing

Investment returns and tax are inseparable. Several strategies can meaningfully improve your after-tax outcomes:

  • Hold growth assets for more than 12 months to access the 50% capital gains tax (CGT) discount for individuals.
  • Utilise franking credits from Australian shares — particularly valuable if your marginal tax rate is high.
  • Invest inside superannuation where earnings are taxed at just 15% (or 0% in the pension phase), significantly lower than most individuals' marginal rates.
  • Consider the ownership structure — investments held in a lower-income spouse's name, a family trust, or a company may be taxed more efficiently depending on your circumstances.

Tax should never be the sole driver of an investment decision, but ignoring it is equally unwise. Structuring investments tax-efficiently from the outset can make a substantial difference to your net wealth over a 10-to-20-year period.
