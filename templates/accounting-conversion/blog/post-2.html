For many {{SUBURB}} small business owners, the Business Activity Statement (BAS) is a source of ongoing confusion and low-level dread. Quarterly deadlines roll around, the forms look complex, and there's always a nagging uncertainty about whether you've included everything correctly. This guide aims to take the mystery out of BAS — what it is, when it's due, what goes in it, and the mistakes that trip up otherwise careful business owners.

What Is a BAS?

A Business Activity Statement is the form you lodge with the ATO to report and pay your tax obligations. Depending on your business, this may include:

  • GST — the goods and services tax you've collected from customers, minus the GST credits you've paid on business purchases
  • PAYG withholding — tax withheld from employee wages
  • PAYG income tax instalments — prepayments toward your annual income tax liability
  • Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT) instalments — if applicable
  • Luxury car tax and wine equalisation tax — if relevant to your industry

For most {{SUBURB}} small businesses, BAS is primarily about GST and, if you have employees, PAYG withholding. The other items apply in more specific circumstances.

Who Needs to Lodge a BAS?

You are required to lodge a BAS if you are registered for GST. GST registration is compulsory once your business has an annual turnover of $75,000 or more (or $150,000 for non-profit organisations). Below this threshold, registration is voluntary — though there are situations where it makes sense to register early, particularly if you have significant input tax credits to claim.

If you have employees, you will also be lodging BAS (or an IAS — Instalment Activity Statement) to report and pay PAYG withholding, even if you are not GST registered.

Quarterly Deadlines

Most small businesses lodge BAS quarterly. The standard due dates for 2025–26 are:

  • Q1 (July–September): 28 October
  • Q2 (October–December): 28 February
  • Q3 (January–March): 28 April
  • Q4 (April–June): 28 July

If you lodge through a registered BAS agent or tax agent (like {{BUSINESS_NAME}}), you are typically granted an extended due date — usually an additional 4 weeks. This is a significant practical benefit of working with a registered agent.

Monthly lodgement applies if your GST turnover exceeds $20 million, and is also available as an option for businesses that prefer more frequent reconciliation.

What to Include in Your BAS

The core of your BAS is the GST calculation. You need to report:

  • G1 — Total sales: All sales (including GST-free and input-taxed sales), not just taxable sales
  • G2 — Export sales: If applicable
  • G3 — Other GST-free sales: Including fresh food, medical services, education
  • G10 — Capital purchases: Business assets purchased during the period
  • G11 — Non-capital purchases: Day-to-day business expenses that include GST

The resulting figure is either a GST payment to the ATO or a refund. Your accounting software (Xero, MYOB, QuickBooks) should populate most of this automatically if your transactions are categorised correctly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The errors we see most frequently when {{SUBURB}} businesses come to us with BAS problems:

  • Claiming GST on GST-free expenses — wages, bank interest, insurance stamp duty, and some professional services don't include GST
  • Omitting private use adjustments — if you use a vehicle or phone for both business and personal use, you can only claim the business portion
  • Not reconciling to your bank account — always cross-check your BAS figures against actual bank transactions before lodging
  • Forgetting PAYG withholding — employee wages are a significant compliance obligation that must be reported even if the net GST result is nil
  • Lodging with uncategorised transactions — transactions sitting as "uncategorised" in your accounting software mean your figures are incomplete

Penalties for Late Lodgement

The ATO does not give informal extensions. If you miss your BAS due date without a formal deferral in place, a failure to lodge (FTL) penalty applies. The penalty is calculated as one penalty unit (currently $330) per 28-day period (or part thereof) that the lodgement is overdue, up to a maximum of five penalty units for small businesses. So a BAS that is two months late can cost you $660 — on top of any interest on late payment.

The ATO also charges the general interest charge (GIC) on any unpaid amount from the due date. GIC rates are set quarterly and are currently above 11% per annum — higher than most business borrowing costs.
