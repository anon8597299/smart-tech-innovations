When to See a GP vs Wait It Out: A Guide for {{SUBURB}} Residents

By {{BUSINESS_NAME}}  ·  {{SUBURB}}, {{STATE}}  ·  Health Advice

Knowing when to book an appointment and when to rest at home is genuinely a skill — and one that most people are never taught. For {{SUBURB}} residents, the question comes up constantly: "Is this something I need to see a GP about, or am I fine to wait?" The stakes go in both directions. Waiting too long on a serious symptom can lead to complications. But booking a GP appointment for something that would resolve on its own within 48 hours adds pressure to an already stretched system — and costs you time and money.

This guide is designed to help you make that call with more confidence. These are general guidelines, not medical advice. If you are ever genuinely unsure, call your clinic directly — most GP practices, including ours, have staff who can help you triage over the phone.

Symptoms That Need Same-Day Attention

Some symptoms should prompt you to call your GP and specifically request a same-day or urgent appointment. These are not emergencies that require an ambulance, but they need professional assessment within hours — not days.

  • Chest tightness or pressure that is new, unexplained, or comes with shortness of breath
  • High fever above 39°C, particularly in children under two, adults over 65, or anyone who is immunocompromised
  • Sudden severe pain — particularly headache described as "the worst of your life", severe abdominal pain, or back pain with urinary symptoms
  • Difficulty breathing that is new or worsening beyond your normal baseline
  • Signs of infection — rapidly spreading redness, warmth, or swelling around a wound or insect bite
  • Mental health crisis — thoughts of self-harm or significant deterioration that cannot wait for a scheduled appointment

When you call, describe these symptoms clearly. A good GP clinic will prioritise you or direct you to the right care pathway.

Symptoms That Can Wait 1–3 Days

Many common presentations do not require urgent care, especially in otherwise healthy adults. These can generally wait for a regular appointment slot:

  • Mild cold or flu symptoms — runny nose, sore throat, mild body aches — in a healthy adult with no underlying conditions
  • Minor skin rash that is not spreading rapidly, not on the face, and not associated with breathing difficulty
  • Recurring headaches that follow your usual pattern and have no new characteristics
  • Mild urinary symptoms in adult women without fever, back pain, or blood in urine
  • Medication refills for stable, ongoing conditions where you have enough supply to cover a few days
  • Minor sprains or strains with full movement and manageable pain

This does not mean dismissing these concerns — it means there is time to book properly, prepare, and have a thorough appointment rather than a rushed urgent slot.

When to Go to Emergency (vs Your GP)

Your GP is not equipped to manage medical emergencies, and emergency departments are not designed for non-urgent presentations. Knowing the difference helps both you and the healthcare system.

Go straight to emergency (000 or nearest ED) for:

  • Suspected stroke — face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty
  • Suspected heart attack — chest pain, jaw pain, left arm pain with sweating
  • Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis)
  • Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
  • Seizures, especially a first-ever seizure
  • Severe breathing difficulty or coughing up blood
  • Serious injury — suspected fractures, deep lacerations, head injury with confusion

Emergency departments exist for genuine emergencies. For everything else, your GP can almost always provide better, more personalised care — usually faster than the wait at an ED.

Chronic Conditions — When Your GP Should Know

If you have a chronic condition — diabetes, hypertension, asthma, heart disease, or mental health conditions — the bar for contacting your GP should be lower than for someone without these conditions. Things worth booking for include:

  • Blood pressure readings consistently outside your normal range at home
  • Noticeable side effects from a new or recently adjusted medication
  • Blood sugar readings that are consistently elevated or erratic
  • Worsening mental health symptoms, even if they feel manageable day-to-day
  • Any new symptom that your specialist has told you to watch for

Chronic condition management is one of the most valuable things a regular GP relationship provides. Don't wait for a crisis — check in regularly.

Making the Most of Your GP Appointment in {{SUBURB}}

Once you've decided to book, a little preparation goes a long way. GPs in busy clinics are working with limited time per patient, so helping them help you is worthwhile:

  • Write down your symptoms before you arrive — when they started, how they've changed, what makes them better or worse
  • Bring a current medication list, including any supplements, over-the-counter medications, or treatments from other practitioners
  • Be upfront about everything — GPs see thousands of patients and there is nothing that surprises them. Honest information leads to better care
  • Ask your questions directly — if you leave the appointment unsure what to do next, you haven't gotten the full value of the visit
  • Mention all your concerns at the start of the appointment, not just one — this helps your GP prioritise and manage time effectively

When in doubt, the right answer is almost always to call your GP and let them help you decide. That's precisely what we are here for.
