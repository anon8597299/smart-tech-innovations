The basics

Deep cycle batteries are the key component in solar energy systems that require electricity storage. Think of a battery as a storage vessel — it holds the power your solar panels produce and releases it when the sun isn't shining.

A battery bank can provide a relatively constant source of power when the grid is down, or during periods when your solar system isn't producing. While batteries aren't 100% efficient, they're predictable and stable enough for reliable long-term service.

Think of it this way

Imagine your battery bank as a bucket of energy. Voltage is the pressure, amperage is the flow rate. Just like a bucket with a small hole needs slightly more water poured in than it holds — a battery takes slightly more energy to charge than it stores. The size of the bucket is the amp hour (Ah) capacity of your battery bank.

How it works

How batteries store energy

Battery technology hasn't changed dramatically in 100 years. The standard construction floods lead plates in sulfuric acid. The chemical reaction between the positively charged lead plate and the negatively charged acid is what stores and releases electricity.

The thickness of the lead plate is closely related to battery lifespan — the positive plate gradually wears away over time due to "positive grid corrosion." Deep cycle batteries use thicker plates, which translates to longer life.

Amp-Hour Rating

All deep cycle batteries are rated in amp-hours (Ah). This tells you how much power is available when discharged evenly over 20 hours. A 100Ah battery can deliver 5 amps for 20 hours, or 10 amps for 10 hours.

Watch out for this

Some manufacturers rate batteries at 100 hours instead of 20 to make them look better on paper. Always check the C-rate when comparing battery specs — insist on a 20-hour (C20) rating for accurate comparison.

Charging process

The 3 stages of charging

Every time your batteries charge, they go through three distinct stages. Your charge controller manages this automatically — but understanding what's happening helps you spot problems early.

Stage 1

Bulk

Maximum current flows in. Takes batteries from empty to ~80–90% charge. The fastest stage.

Stage 2

Absorption

Voltage peaks and stabilises. Current tapers off as internal resistance rises. Finishing the charge.

Stage 3

Float

Voltage drops to a maintenance level. Prevents overcharge and gassing. Keeps batteries topped up.

Battery types

Which battery type is right for you?

There are four main battery types used in residential solar. Each has different costs, maintenance requirements, and performance characteristics. Here's the plain-English comparison.

Flooded Lead Acid

The most common battery in solar systems. Longest track record, lowest cost per amp-hour. Requires regular watering, equalising charges and terminal cleaning.

Lowest cost 5–15 yr lifespan Needs maintenance

Sealed AGM

Absorbed Glass Mat batteries are sealed, maintenance-free and can hold 1.5× the amp-hour capacity of a similar flooded battery. Better for enclosed locations. Higher upfront cost.

Maintenance-free High density Higher cost

Gel Sealed

Uses silica to gel the electrolyte, reducing gassing and spillage. Must be charged slower than flooded or AGM — overcharging causes gas pockets that permanently reduce capacity.

Low maintenance Slow charge only Being phased out

Lithium-Ion

Smaller, lighter, longer lifespan, better depth of discharge. Think Tesla Powerwall. Higher upfront cost — but over its lifetime, comparable to sealed lead-acid. Price is falling as production scales.

Longest lifespan 80%+ discharge Highest upfront
Lifespan

What kills batteries early

Battery failures are rarely the fault of the batteries themselves. The most common causes of premature failure are preventable with proper setup and maintenance.

Common causes of early battery failure

  • Loss of electrolyte due to heat or overcharging
  • Chronic undercharging — never reaching 100% state of charge
  • Discharging past 50–80% depth regularly
  • Using tap water instead of distilled water (flooded batteries)
  • Freezing temperatures or excessive heat exposure
  • Terminal corrosion left untreated
  • More than 3 parallel battery strings (uneven equalisation)
Pro tip

Batteries that are protected from temperature extremes, kept clean, and regularly brought to full charge can last 10+ years with minimal intervention. The secret is a proper initial installation — not daily effort.

Maintenance

Simple maintenance checklist

For flooded batteries, basic maintenance takes minutes and adds years to battery life. Sealed (AGM, Gel, Lithium) batteries require virtually no user maintenance.

Flooded battery maintenance routine

  • Check electrolyte levels every 1–2 months — plates must stay submerged
  • Top up with distilled water only — never tap water, never acid
  • Wash battery tops twice a year with baking soda solution to remove acid spatter
  • Apply petroleum jelly (Vaseline) to terminals to prevent corrosion
  • Bring batteries to 100% charge at least every 3 weeks
  • Check and tighten all cable connections every 6 months
Safety warning

Battery acid is hazardous. Always wear safety glasses when working around batteries. Keep a bottle of baking soda and water solution nearby to neutralise any acid splashes or spills immediately. Don't wear anything you'd mind ruining.

Sizing your system

How to size your battery bank

Getting the size right is the most important decision in a battery-backed solar system. Too small and you'll constantly drain them flat. Too large and they'll never reach a full charge.

The 5-day rule

As a minimum, size your battery bank to hold 5 days of load. Most home power systems grow over time, so err on the larger side. Important: after 1 year of service, you cannot simply add new batteries to an existing bank — battery voltage response changes with age and mixed banks fail faster.

Solar array sizing

Your solar array (if it's the primary energy source) should be sized to produce 30% more energy than your average load requires. This compensates for battery losses and below-average charging days. Unlike battery banks, a solar array can be expanded at any time.

Weekend home tip

If your property sits empty during the week, consider a larger battery bank — you'll have 5+ days of solar charging time with no load. Alternatively, if you have a generator backup, the battery bank can be slightly undersized since you can run the generator to top up when needed.

Avoid more than 3 parallel strings

The ideal battery bank is a single series of cells. Under no circumstances should you install more than three parallel battery strings — the bank will lose equalisation and weak cells will steal from surrounding cells, accelerating failure across the entire bank.

Monitoring

Know what your battery is doing

Would you drive a car with no dashboard? A battery monitor isn't a luxury — it's a necessity. There are three ways to measure battery state of charge, in order of accuracy:

State of charge methods

  • Voltage meter — Least accurate. Affected by current flow, temperature and time. Cheap and simple but unreliable for precise readings.
  • Hydrometer — Most accurate for flooded batteries. Measures electrolyte density cell by cell. Definitive, but only works on flooded (wet) batteries.
  • Amp-hour meter — Most user-friendly. Tracks all power in and out over time. The only definitive method for sealed batteries. Recommended for all systems.
